Introduction, Background & Problem Definition

An electrocardiogram (ECG) uses 12 leads to measure body surface voltages, aiding physicians in diagnosing heart diseases and arrhythmias. [1] An electrocardiogram (ECG) uses 12 leads to measure body surface voltages, aiding physicians in diagnosing heart diseases and arrhythmias. The essence of an ECG is captured in its ability to graphically display the electrical activity of the heart over a period of time. This graphical representation, composed of waveforms, serves as a window into the heart's operational state. The primary waveforms, collectively known as the "P-QRS-T" sequence, represent different phases of the heart's rhythm and cycle (Fig. 1). Simplifying these, the P-wave indicates the initial contraction of the atria, the QRS complex signifies the subsequent contraction of the ventricles, and the T-wave reflects the recovery phase. These waveforms, continuously produced as the heart beats, form a detailed map of cardiac activity. Anomalies in this activity, such as arrhythmias, manifest as deviations in the waveform patterns, sequence, or directionality, providing critical diagnostic clues. [2][3]

Fig 1. Generic ECG waveform for a singular heart beat. [4]

Historically, the electrocardiogram has been a cornerstone in the diagnosis of cardiac ailments. By measuring the electrical voltages from various points on the body, ECGs unveil the intricacies of cardiac function and highlight abnormalities that may indicate conditions like atrial fibrillation. The ECG achieves this through 12 distinct leads, each offering a unique perspective on the heart's electrical activity, akin to viewing the heart from different angles in three-dimensional space. These leads are categorized into limb leads (I, II, III, aVR, aVL, aVF) and precordial leads (V1, V2, V3, V4, V5, V6), each providing essential data on the direction and sequence of cardiac activation. Our project zeroes in on leads II and V6 for initial analysis. This choice is grounded in these leads' proven efficacy in capturing critical aspects of cardiac activity. Lead II, for example, offers a direct view of the heart's electrical axis, making it particularly sensitive to arrhythmias and other cardiac abnormalities. By focusing on these leads, our study aims to harness their combined diagnostic value, maximizing the potential to detect and classify a wide spectrum of cardiac arrhythmias and conditions. [5]

Since the 1970s, deterministic computer programs have helped with ECG analysis, but recent advancements in machine learning, especially deep learning [6]–[9] offer superior accuracy. [10] For example, a recent model achieved an F1 score of 99.99% for heartbeat abnormality classification. [11] Despite these advances, clinical adoption of these mostly black-box models remains limited. [12], [13] To increase acceptance by physicians, classification models of heart conditions are needed that are explainable (perhaps through a saliency map) and that give well-calibrated probabilistic classifications. Sophisticated models have been known to flounder in production. [14] To earn the trust of physicians, the uncertainty of models must be addressed. [15] To our knowledge, machine learning-based interpretations of ECGs today fail to inform physicians of the uncertainty in their predictions. Doctors need more than a label - they need to know if they can trust it.

Despite the existence of benchmark datasets like the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database [16] and the Physionet Database, challenges remain in developing models that rival human cardiologist performance. These challenges are partly due to limited sample diversity and arrhythmia types in available datasets. Moreover, addressing multi-label diagnosis issues is crucial as it reflects real-world clinical scenarios where patients often present with multiple concurrent cardiac diseases. Such complexities necessitate models capable of handling intricate diagnosis scenarios, moving beyond the singular label predictions to accommodate the multifaceted nature of cardiac conditions encountered in clinical practice.

To meet these objectives, we obtained an open-access de-identified dataset of 45,152 patients of 12-lead ECG signals from PhysioNet, a collaboration involving Chapman University, Shaoxing People’s Hospital (Shaoxing Hospital Zhejiang University School of Medicine), and Ningbo First Hospital. [17]-[19] The dataset, meticulously assembled under these institutions' guidance, encompasses a comprehensive collection of 12-lead ECGs, each sampled at a 500 Hz rate. This extensive dataset not only includes multiple common rhythms but also features additional cardiovascular conditions, enriching the scope of our analysis. Each ECG record in this database is accompanied by header information on the patient’s sex, age, prescriptions, etc. Importantly, each ECG was labeled with corresponding SNOMED CT codes by professional experts, covering over 80 distinct cardiac anomalies and arrhythmias. This rigorous classification enables our project to delve into a broad spectrum of cardiac abnormalities, laying the groundwork for developing a nuanced machine learning model capable of diagnosing a wide array of heart conditions.

Methods

Data Preprocessing & Feature Extraction

In this section, we detail our systematic approach adopted to prepare/denoise the ECG data for subsequent analysis and the extraction of pertinent features critical for diagnosing abnormalities and arrhythmias. The preprocessing and feature extraction tasks were implemented in Python, utilizing libraries such as SciPy, NumPy, neurokit2, PyWavelets, and scikit-learn.

The electrical signal emitted by the heart as recorded by the leads of an ECG can be contaminated by various sources of noise and non-clinically relevant anomalies. For example, as discussed in [20], power line interference at 50 or 60 Hz, baseline wander, and muscle twitching can degrade the signal. Fortunately, our dataset has some redundancy to guard against excessive noise in a single lead (our dataset has 12 leads). Each lead can be thought of as recording a different view of the heart as explained in [21]. Additionally, since the sampling frequency for our dataset is 500 Hz and the electrical signal of the heart is estimated to travel through the body at a speed of 1500 m/s, which is not detectable until a sampling rate of 1 kHz, [22] each lead voltage is effectively measuring the same activity of the heart without any time delay.

Data Preprocessing & Denoising. During the data preprocessing phase, we originally applied a series of filters and techniques to the raw ECG signals to mitigate noise and artifacts that could potentially impede the model's performance. Specifically, the signals underwent detrending to remove baseline wander, followed by bandpass filtering (with cutoff frequencies set at 0.5 Hz and 45 Hz) to retain only the pertinent frequency components of the ECG signal. Furthermore, a notch filter at 50 Hz was employed to eliminate power line noise, and wavelet denoising, leveraging a 'db5' wavelet, was applied to suppress high-frequency noise while preserving the essential features of the ECG signal. In instances of pronounced noise, a Savitzky-Golay filter was utilized to smooth the signals further, thereby facilitating a more accurate delineation of the cardiac cycles. However, upon further inspection, we realized that a more streamlined approach would suffice for the initial preprocessing requirements of our project.

Our final preprocessing steps included signal detrending using the "tarvainen2002" method to effectively remove baseline wander without overly smoothing the ECG waveform - this choice was motivated by the method's ability to preserve the morphology of the ECG signal. Additionally, we kept the notch filter to eliminate power line interference.

Feature Extraction. The feature extraction process is designed to capture the intricate dynamics and characteristics of the ECG signals, which are pivotal for the accurate diagnosis of cardiac abnormalities. Later on, we plan on experimenting with neural networks that automatically extract relevant features, but we also chose an initial manual approach for comparison. Utilizing the NeuroKit2 library, we identify the critical peaks (P, Q, R, S, T) within each ECG cycle. The identification process involves an adaptive delineation method, optimized for precision in detecting these peaks even amidst varying signal qualities and arrhythmias. This phase is critical, as the precise localization of these peaks serves as the foundation for calculating a variety of derived features crucial for the diagnostic process. Moreover, through our exploration of other feature extraction techniques appropriate for ECGs, we discovered a superior method for the extraction of R peaks using visibility graphs.

In implementing this refined R-peak detection technique, we integrated a visibility graph-based method, utilizing the Fast Nonlinear Visibility Graph (FastNVG) algorithm.

Fig 3. Example Time Series Maxima detection using visibility graphs. [23]

This method transforms the ECG signal into a graph domain, where the detection of R peaks becomes a graph-based problem, allowing for a more nuanced and accurate identification of these critical points. Specifically, for each patient's ECG signal, the algorithm processes the data to identify R peaks, effectively handling the signal's nonlinear characteristics and noise, which are inherent challenges in ECG analysis

Following the peak detection, we calculate several intervals and segments that are essential for diagnosing cardiac conditions, such as RR, PP, QT intervals, ST segments, QRS durations, P-wave durations, and PR segments. These calculations are based on the identified peaks and are pivotal for the subsequent arrhythmia classification and abnormality detection.

Supplementing missing values. Moreover, to address potential missing values and inaccuracies in peak detection due to signal noise or arrhythmias, we implemented a correction mechanism that infers the positions of missing peaks based on signal morphology and expected locations relative to detected peaks. This mechanism includes backward searching for P peaks from Q peaks or R peaks, forward searching for S peaks, and a dynamic search for T peaks based on the proximity of S and the following P peak. These corrections ensure the reliability of the extracted features, which is paramount for the integrity of the machine learning model's input data.

Fig 4. Feature extraction and denoising for sample ECGs.

Anomaly detection. Given the nature of the ECG waveforms and the large quantity of files in our dataset, our QRS extraction techniques would occasionally produce inaccurate features. Some common issues include mislabeling of peaks or skipping peaks altogether. We were able to mitigate this risk by discarding data where the quantity of the QRS values did not accurately reflect the number of heartbeats detected in the ecg waveform. Additionally, some ecg recordings begin or end halfway through a heartbeat, which disrupts the QRS extraction. To circumvent this issue we decided to clip the first and last heartbeat of every ECG waveform. Lastly, the number of heartbeats in each ecg waveform is not consistent, especially in patients with anomalous heart rhythms. So we resized every feature row to equal the size of the largest sample, such that each resized array is filled with repeated copies of the features in the original array.

ML Algorithms/Models Implemented

As a first attempt to validate our extracted features, we opted to perform GMM clustering into two labels, to evaluate if the feature set is automatically grouped by “healthy” vs “unhealthy”. Our second attempt to validate our featureset, we trained a basic Random Forest on a subset of 3 arrhythmia labels in the dataset.

The first attempt involved comparing pairs of observations and creating a dissimilarity matrix between them. To accomplish this, we turned to the foundational technique of Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) for comparing time series. In DTW, the times are allowed to “warp” so that the signals between the two series in comparison become close to one another (in the L2 sense). We used the dependent multivariate version of DTW to account for all 12 leads. Additionally, to make sure we were comparing apples to apples, we first segmented the ECGs by heart beat (a period of time from one R peak to the next). The mean DTW was used for all pairwise comparisons of the segments. Naturally, with many pairwise comparisons, this DTW approach is rather slow, taking about 0.7 seconds per pair of ECGs.

Dissimilarity matrices produced by DTW can fail to satisfy the triangle inequality, which means that two ECGs can be “far” from each other and yet both can be “close” to a third ECG. This can cause clusters to be nonsensical if the ECGs that are “far” from each other are assigned the same cluster because of their similarity to a third ECG. To account for this, we coded a state-of-the-art-algorithm for sparse metric repair (​iteratively reweighted ℓ1 minimization) that attempts to minimally perturb a dissimilarity matrix into a distance matrix, as laid out in algorithm 6 from [24], along with the optimizations provided in it.

 We then attempted to use multidimensional scaling (MDS) to get actual data points from our distance matrix. The result of MDS was then fed into a GMM.

Results, Discussion & Next Steps

Results & Discussion

As a first attempt to validate our extracted features, we opted to perform GMM clustering into two labels, to evaluate if the feature set is automatically grouped by “healthy” vs “unhealthy”. 70%~ of the resulting clusters align correctly with the healthy/unhealthy labels. However we discovered that our dataset is skewed in this regard, as it has several more “unhealthy” samples than “healthy”, so the 70% could easily be no better than random guessing.

Fig 6. GMM: 5000 samples with 70%~ “correctly” labeled

Fig 7. GMM: 10000 samples with ~73% “correctly” labeled

As a second attempt to validate our featureset, we trained a basic Random Forest on a subset of 3 arrhythmia labels in the dataset. Soon after training our first model we discovered that the labels in our dataset are not equally distributed. The first Random Forest model had 80% accuracy, but the subset we used to train with contained 64% “Sinus Bradycardia” labels, so it was likely just as good as random guessing for the other labels.  

Fig 8. KDE plot of Low QRS voltages and Counterclockwise Cardiac Rotation

A Kernel Density Estimation is used for estimating the PDF of a variable. The kernel function applied is a Gaussian Distribution, and an optimized bandwidth of 3 was chosen. The KDE computes the estimate by summing up the kernel functions at each data point and then normalizing the results by the total number of points and bandwidth. The KDE has been run on all of our SNOMED labels besides a few that do not have enough data. On the left, the KDE for Counterclockwise Cardiac Rotation shows a similar distribution in men and women, with the condition peaking in frequency around 70 years old. The right side shows a distribution that may slightly impact men more, but it can be clearly seen that the condition tends to become more frequent later in age in men vs. women.

Fig 9. Confusion matrix with 4413 equally distributed samples with 62.54% accuracy

Random Forest. Our largest Random Forest test involved analyzing 4,413 samples, evenly distributed across three categories, yielding a model accuracy of 62.54%. This performance is only slightly above random chance. Looking at the confusion matrix “Sinus Bradycardia” seems to do much better.  Going forward we will need to take a second look at our feature extraction methods and explore ways to improve them. Specifically we will try to understand why the “Sinus Bradycardia” labeling performed better.

Next Steps

In response to our preliminary assessment of the features we've extracted, we intend to explore the following:

  1. Improve ECG Feature Extraction: We will need to investigate our current methods of feature extraction and explore potential new features.
  2. Improve ECG Preprocessing Prior to DTW: We may also experiment with other models than GMM as the projection to 2-dimensional space by the prior step of MDS may be causing us to lose information.
  3. Assessment of Feature Effectiveness: We will identify the weakest features with and either improve or eliminate them from our featureset. This may involve discarding data with insufficient labels to maintain a balanced dataset.
  4. Inclusion of Multiple Cardiac Signals: We will attempt to extract features from various leads to set how it affects the performance of our model.
  5. CNN Implementation: We plan to train two distinct CNN models; the first will use raw pixel data from lead II of ECGs, while the second will use the extracted featureset. We will compare & contrast the efficacy of both models.
  6. RNN (LSTM) Implementation: We plan to explore the deployment of RNNs, with a specific focus on Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, for the purpose of capturing the temporal dependencies and dynamics present in ECG signal sequences. This approach is anticipated to significantly bolster our model's ability to understand and predict sequential patterns, thereby enhancing its diagnostic accuracy.
  7. DTW Clustering. Our resulting clusters were not so well-defined. For evaluation purposes, we need a metric that takes clustering probabilities, multi-labels and outputs a value. Upon researching, we have decided to pursue a method called Label Ranking Average Precision (LRAP). LRAP is a ranking-based metric that can be adapted for evaluating how well the GMM clustering captures the hierarchy or order of labels in multi-label classification. This could involve assessing the probability distributions over clusters in relation to the relevance of different labels for each instance. We aim to use the LRAP score to evaluate our clusters.

  1. Introduction, Background & Problem Definition

An electrocardiogram (ECG) uses 12 leads to measure body surface voltages, aiding physicians in diagnosing heart diseases and arrhythmias. 1 An ECG graphically displays the electrical activity of the heart over a period of time. This graphical representation, composed of waveforms, serves as a window into the heart's operational state. The primary waveforms, collectively known as the "P-QRS-T" sequence, represent different phases of the heart's rhythm and cycle (Fig. 1). Simplifying these, the P-wave indicates the initial contraction of the atria, the QRS complex signifies the subsequent contraction of the ventricles, and the T-wave reflects the recovery phase. These waveforms, continuously produced as the heart beats, form a detailed map of cardiac activity. Anomalies in this activity, such as arrhythmias, manifest as deviations in the waveform patterns, sequence, or directionality, providing critical diagnostic clues. 2–4

Figure 1. Generic ECG waveform for a singular heart beat. 4

Historically, the electrocardiogram has been a cornerstone in the diagnosis of cardiac ailments. By measuring the electrical voltages from various points on the body, ECGs unveil the intricacies of cardiac function and highlight abnormalities that may indicate conditions like atrial fibrillation. The ECG achieves this through 12 distinct leads, each offering a unique perspective on the heart's electrical activity, akin to viewing the heart from different angles in three-dimensional space. These leads are categorized into limb leads (I, II, III, aVR, aVL, aVF) and precordial leads (V1, V2, V3, V4, V5, V6), each providing essential data on the direction and sequence of cardiac activation. Our project zeroes in on leads II and V6 for initial analysis. This choice is grounded in these leads' proven efficacy in capturing critical aspects of cardiac activity. Lead II, for example, offers a direct view of the heart's electrical axis, making it particularly sensitive to arrhythmias and other cardiac abnormalities. 5 By focusing on these leads, our study aims to harness their combined diagnostic value, maximizing the potential to detect and classify a wide spectrum of cardiac arrhythmias and conditions.

Since the 1970s, deterministic computer programs have helped with ECG analysis, but recent advancements in machine learning, especially deep learning 6–9 offer superior accuracy. 10 For example, a recent model achieved an F1 score of 99.99% for heartbeat abnormality classification. 11 Despite these advances, clinical adoption of these mostly black-box models remains limited. 12,13 To increase acceptance by physicians, classification models of heart conditions are needed that are explainable and that give well-calibrated probabilistic classifications. Sophisticated models have been known to flounder in production. 14 To earn the trust of physicians, the uncertainty of models must be addressed. 15 To our knowledge, machine learning-based interpretations of ECGs today fail to inform physicians of the uncertainty in their predictions. Doctors need more than a label - they need to know if they can trust it.

Despite the existence of benchmark datasets like the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database 16 and the Physionet Database 17, challenges remain in developing models that rival human cardiologist performance. These challenges are partly due to limited sample diversity and arrhythmia types in available datasets. Moreover, addressing multi-label diagnosis issues is crucial as it reflects real-world clinical scenarios where patients often present with multiple concurrent cardiac diseases. Such complexities necessitate models capable of handling intricate diagnosis scenarios, moving beyond the singular label predictions to accommodate the multifaceted nature of cardiac conditions encountered in clinical practice.

Dataset. To meet these objectives, we obtained an open-access de-identified dataset of 45,152 patients of 12-lead ECG signals from PhysioNet, a collaboration involving Chapman University, Shaoxing People’s Hospital (Shaoxing Hospital Zhejiang University School of Medicine), and Ningbo First Hospital. 17–19 The dataset, meticulously assembled under these institutions' guidance, encompasses a comprehensive collection of 12-lead ECGs, each sampled at a 500 Hz rate. This extensive dataset not only includes multiple common rhythms but also features additional cardiovascular conditions, enriching the scope of our analysis. Each ECG record in this database is accompanied by header information on the patient’s sex, age, prescriptions, etc. Importantly, each ECG was labeled with corresponding SNOMED CT codes by professional experts, covering over 80 distinct cardiac anomalies and arrhythmias. This rigorous classification enables our project to delve into a broad spectrum of cardiac abnormalities, laying the groundwork for developing a machine learning model capable of diagnosing a wide array of heart conditions.

  1. Methods

2.1. Data Processing & Feature Extraction

In this section, we detail our systematic approach adopted to prepare/denoise the ECG data for subsequent analysis and the extraction of pertinent features critical for diagnosing abnormalities and arrhythmias. The preprocessing and feature extraction tasks were implemented in Python, utilizing libraries such as SciPy, NumPy, neurokit2, PyWavelets, and scikit-learn.

The electrical signal emitted by the heart as recorded by the leads of an ECG can be contaminated by various sources of noise and non-clinically relevant anomalies. For example, as discussed in 20, power line interference at 50 or 60 Hz, baseline wander, and muscle twitching can degrade the signal. Fortunately, our dataset has some redundancy to guard against excessive noise in a single lead (our dataset has 12 leads). Each lead can be thought of as recording a different view of the heart as explained in 21. Additionally, since the sampling frequency for our dataset is 500 Hz and the electrical signal of the heart is estimated to travel through the body at a speed of 1500 m/s, which is not detectable until a sampling rate of 1 kHz, 22 each lead voltage is effectively measuring the same activity of the heart without any time delay.

Data Preprocessing & Denoising. During the data preprocessing phase, we originally applied a series of filters and techniques to the raw ECG signals to mitigate noise and artifacts that could potentially impede the model's performance. Specifically, the signals underwent detrending to remove baseline wander, followed by bandpass filtering (with cutoff frequencies set at 0.5 Hz and 45 Hz) to retain only the pertinent frequency components of the ECG signal. Furthermore, a notch filter at 50 Hz was employed to eliminate power line noise, and wavelet denoising, leveraging a 'db5' wavelet, was applied to suppress high-frequency noise while preserving the essential features of the ECG signal. In instances of pronounced noise, a Savitzky-Golay filter was utilized to smooth the signals further, thereby facilitating a more accurate delineation of the cardiac cycles. However, upon further inspection, we realized that a more streamlined approach would suffice for the initial preprocessing requirements of our project.

Our final preprocessing steps included signal detrending using the "tarvainen2002" method 23 to effectively remove baseline wander without overly smoothing the ECG waveform - this choice was motivated by the method's ability to preserve the morphology of the ECG signal. Additionally, we kept the notch filter to eliminate power line interference.

Feature Extraction. The feature extraction process is designed to capture the intricate dynamics and characteristics of the ECG signals, which are pivotal for the accurate diagnosis of cardiac abnormalities. Later on, we plan on experimenting with neural networks that automatically extract relevant features, but we also chose an initial manual approach for comparison. Utilizing the NeuroKit2 library, we identify the critical peaks (P, Q, R, S, T) within each ECG cycle. The identification process involves an adaptive delineation method, optimized for precision in detecting these peaks even amidst varying signal qualities and arrhythmias. This phase is critical, as the precise localization of these peaks serves as the foundation for calculating a variety of derived features crucial for the diagnostic process. Moreover, through our exploration of other feature extraction techniques appropriate for ECGs, we discovered a superior method for the extraction of R peaks using visibility graphs.

In implementing this R-peak detection technique, we integrated a visibility graph-based method, utilizing the Fast Nonlinear Visibility Graph (FastNVG) algorithm. 24 

Figure 3. Example of time series peak-finding using visibility graphs. 25

This method transforms the ECG signal into a graph domain, where the detection of R peaks becomes a graph-based problem, allowing for a more nuanced and accurate identification of these critical points. Specifically, for each patient's ECG signal, the algorithm processes the data to identify R peaks, effectively handling the signal's nonlinear characteristics and noise, which are inherent challenges in ECG analysis

Following the peak detection, we calculate several intervals and segments that are essential for diagnosing cardiac conditions, such as RR, PP, QT intervals, ST segments, QRS durations, P-wave durations, and PR segments. These calculations are based on the identified peaks and are pivotal for the subsequent arrhythmia classification and abnormality detection.

Supplementing missing values. Moreover, to address potential missing values and inaccuracies in peak detection due to signal noise or arrhythmias, we implemented a correction mechanism that infers the positions of missing peaks based on signal morphology and expected locations relative to detected peaks. This mechanism includes backward searching for P peaks from Q peaks or R peaks, forward searching for S peaks, and a dynamic search for T peaks based on the proximity of S and the following P peak. These corrections ensure the reliability of the extracted features, which is paramount for the integrity of the machine learning model's input data.

Figure 4. Feature extraction and denoising for sample ECGs.

Anomaly detection. Given the nature of the ECG waveforms and the large quantity of files in our dataset, our QRS extraction techniques would occasionally produce inaccurate features. Some common issues include mislabeling of peaks or skipping peaks altogether. We were able to mitigate this risk by discarding data where the quantity of the QRS values did not accurately reflect the number of heartbeats detected in the ecg waveform. Additionally, some ecg recordings begin or end halfway through a heartbeat, which disrupts the QRS extraction. To circumvent this issue we decided to clip the first and last heartbeat of every ECG waveform. Lastly, the number of heartbeats in each ecg waveform is not consistent, especially in patients with anomalous heart rhythms. So we resized every feature row to equal the size of the largest sample, such that each resized array is filled with repeated copies of the features in the original array.

2.2. 2D Variation Maps

In the realm of electrocardiography (ECG), the traditional data format involves capturing electrical voltages across the heart through various leads, generating a time-series output that clinicians interpret. For our project, we use an idea from TimesNet 26 to enhance the analysis and interpretation of ECG data by converting the traditional 1D time-series data into a more visually interpretable and computationally amenable format known as 2D variation maps. Initially, the raw ECG data comprises 5000 voltage measurements for each of the twelve leads, sampled at a rate of 500 Hz over a 10 second period. This data, inherently noisy due to various artifacts and external interferences, undergoes the aforementioned denoising process. Post-denoising, the data is not only cleaner but also more conducive to pattern recognition when transformed into a two-dimensional format.

2.2.1. 2D Variation Maps (without features)

To leverage the spatial feature extraction capabilities of CNNs, we reformat the 1D ECG voltages for each lead into a 50 x 100 2D map, thus creating a 3D array by stacking 12 such maps, each map representing one of the twelve leads. This conversion is done by first normalizing the voltage data to ensure uniformity in scale and range across all leads. The normalization handles potential outliers and non-finite values by replacing them with the mean of finite data points, ensuring that no lead's data skews the results due to scaling discrepancies.

Figure 6. Formation of 3D array composed of 2D Variation Maps.

The conversion from a 1D series to a 2D format involves reshaping the normalized data into a matrix of dimensions 50x100. This transformation is performed by segmenting the lead data into sections that are restructured into two dimensions—where the width is set to 100 data points. The choice of a 50x100 matrix is strategic; it balances the need to maintain a manageable computational complexity with the need to preserve crucial temporal and amplitude resolutions that might be critical for detecting nuanced patterns in the ECG data. The width of 100 units allows the model to capture fine variations within smaller time segments, while the height of 50 units ensures that the entire sequence is comprehensively represented without excessive compression that could lead to loss of vital information.

These 2D maps effectively capture the temporal dynamics and amplitude variations within each ECG lead in a format that a CNN can process to recognize and learn from complex patterns. This method of data representation not only enhances the ability of our neural network model to perform feature extraction more effectively but also aligns with the way CNNs traditionally excel in image recognition tasks. By treating each ECG lead as an "image" of voltage variations over time, the network can utilize its convolutional filters to detect features such as peaks, troughs, and irregular patterns indicative of cardiac abnormalities across multiple labels.

2.2.2. 2D Feature Variation Maps

Alternatively, following a method similar to the one presented in 2.2.1, we construct a new type of 3D array composed of 2D variation maps, but using the extracted features instead. As mentioned in the introduction, a collection of five peaks, namely P, Q, R,S, T, is termed as a wavelet (Fig 1). Within each ECG signal there are multiple wavelet complexes present.

Utilizing the feature extraction and denoising process described in 2.1., we can identify and extract the exact timestamp and voltages for each of the five wavelet peaks present in the ECG signal, and we can therefore create a 2D variation map composed of the timestamps of the five wavelet peaks and a second 2D variation map composed of the corresponding voltages. Hence, both aforementioned 2D feature variation maps are of size 5 x m, where m is the number of wavelets present. Subsequently, we can form a 3D array of size 2 x 5 x m, by stacking the two 2D feature variation maps, as seen in Fig. 7. The first map corresponds to the time stamps for the wavelet points, and the second map is for the voltages.

Figure 7. Formation of 3D array composed of 2D Feature Variation Maps.

Since the number of wavelets m varies from patient to patient, we warp around to pad the values for maps with smaller m making the overall size the same for all patients. The maximum value of wavelets found across all patients was 12, hence each map was made with m = 12. The conversion from a 1D series to a 2D format stays the same as 2.2.1.

It’s important to note that this second type of 3D array is composed of two 2D feature variation maps of a single lead, specifically lead V6, due to its prevalence and for the sake of convenience. In contrast, the type of 3D array described in 2.2.1, is composed of 2D variation maps for all 12 twelve leads without loss of information. Therefore, we expect that the loss of information that comes with using the 2D feature variation maps will be reflected in the model’s performance.

2.3. Unsupervised Learning Method

Do the ECGs follow a learnable pattern? Are there patients with ECGs that are outliers? To answer these questions, we used the unsupervised learning technique for time series of dynamic time warping (DTW). 27 In DTW, the times are allowed to “warp” so that the signals between the two series in comparison become close to one another (in the L2 sense). The closeness of the two signals can then be measured while accounting for timing differences. Ultimately, the squared Euclidean metric was used in our analysis.

Figure 8. Sourced from https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamic_time_warping#/media/File:Dynamic_time_warping.png 

Drawing by Programminglinguist

As DTW can be rather computationally expensive, we used a random subsample of all patients, restricted our attention to the lead II signal, and stratified the patients by age group and sex. Stratification reduced the number of possible comparisons and made the comparisons more meaningful.

Before blindly using DTW, the lead II signals were smoothed and baseline detrended. R peaks were identified in each patient’s lead II signal using visibility graphs. The lead II signals of each patient were then segmented according to the time indices of their respective R peaks. At first, using the squared Euclidean distance in DTW presented some challenges as different patients could have similarly shaped recordings at vastly different raw voltages. We wanted the DTW to focus mostly on the shape comparison and not the voltage differences. So, we converted each voltage measurement in the signal to its corresponding percentile. This allowed the shapes of the P and T waves between the R peaks to be preserved while normalizing the data to make it comparable. For each comparison between pairs of patients in an age/sex strata, a random sample of 20% of the segmented heart beats from each patient were selected.

Dissimilarity matrices produced by DTW can fail to satisfy the triangle inequality, which means that two ECGs can be “far” from each other and yet both can be “close” to a third ECG. This can cause clusters to be nonsensical if the ECGs that are “far” from each other are assigned the same cluster because of their similarity to a third ECG. To account for this, we coded a state-of-the-art-algorithm for sparse metric repair (​iteratively reweighted ℓ1 minimization) from algorithm 6 in 28. The algorithm attempts to minimally perturb a dissimilarity matrix into a distance matrix.

2.4. Supervised Learning Methods

2.4.1. Long Short-Term Memory Network (LSTM)

Model Overview. As suggested by its name, "Long Short-Term Memory," we chose to use an LSTM model because of its capability to retain crucial information from long-term dependencies while simultaneously processing short-term data. At a fundamental level, an LSTM network comprises unit cells, each equipped with a "forget gate," "input gate," "cell state update," and "output gate”. The forget gate is responsible for deciding what information is discarded from the cell state, and various loss layers can be used depending upon the engineer of the model. The input gate decides which values to update using a similar loss function to that of the forget gate. Based upon the information from the forget and input gates, the state of the cell is then sequentially updated, and the output gate decides what information is to be passed on. We opted for an LSTM model due to the critical nature of long-term dependencies in ECG readings - conditions such as ‘bradycardia’, ‘tachycardia’, and ‘sinus rhythm’ depend on the frequency of wavelets. Furthermore, it's essential for the model to detect abrupt changes, such as the missing wavelets characteristic of Atrial Fibrillation. We also found upon research that it has already had much success in reading ECG signals, and due to its prevalence, much documentation was available in case we did run into trouble.

We decided the best way to approach creating an optimal LSTM in the time given was to program 2 different models so we could try different architectures out in parallel and account for the difficulties involved with successfully tuning a single model collaboratively while working remotely. Some collaboration was involved so that we did not try multiple, similar combinations of architectural hyperparameters, but also to provide knowledge of what was working very well. In order to accurately pick which model is best, we used the same testing data and files.

The model takes in a 1x5000 input size (the size of an ecg sample). 2.4.2. Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)

Model Overview. For our project, we also implemented a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to analyze and classify ECG signals. CNNs are particularly well-suited for image and signal processing tasks due to their ability to capture spatial hierarchies in data. By converting the 1D ECG time-series data from each of the twelve leads into 2D variation maps, we transform the ECG signals into a format analogous to images. These 3D arrays of 2D maps, created both with and without additional extracted features, allow the CNN to exploit both local and global features in the ECG data, enhancing its ability to detect complex patterns associated with various cardiac conditions. This approach leverages the strength of CNNs in handling grid-like data, making it an innovative method for ECG classification that capitalizes on the detailed spatial transformations provided by the 2D variation map method.

Model Architecture. The architecture of our CNN (Fig. 11) is tailored to handle the complexity of ECG data effectively. It consists of three convolutional layers followed by batch normalization and pooling layers. Specifically, the first convolutional layer receives the 3D array of 2D ECG maps, which have 12 channels—one for each lead. The network then escalates the feature maps through 64, 128, and 256 filters respectively, each with a kernel size of 3x3 and padding set to maintain dimensionality. Depending on whether the input 3D arrays include features, an adaptive average pooling layer or a max pooling layer is applied after each convolution to reduce dimensionality while retaining significant features. Post convolutional layers, an adaptive pooling layer standardizes the output size, ensuring that it remains consistent regardless of input variations. This output is then flattened and fed into a sequence of fully connected layers with dropout for regularization, culminating in a final layer that predicts the presence or absence of multiple cardiac conditions.

Figure 11. CNN architecture using arrays of 2D variation maps (without features) as inputs.

Dataset Organization. Upon inspection of the dataset, we found a great variability in the number of output labels. For this case, we first sorted the labels in descending order of their loss and selected the top k (3-6) labels for training purposes. Among these, we created another date set that ensures equal representation of each label. This is done to ensure there is no bias towards the majority label, and in order to prevent overfitting. Finally, a simple 80-20 train-test split was used for the training and validation of our model.

Model Training & Optimization. Training our CNN involves using the binary cross-entropy loss function, suitable for multi-label classification tasks where each label is treated independently. We optimize our network using the Adam optimizer, which is effective in managing sparse gradients on noisy datasets such as ECG signals. Crucially, we incorporate weight decay in our optimizer settings—a regularization technique that helps prevent the model from overfitting by adding a penalty on the size of the coefficients. Weight decay limits the complexity of the model by penalizing larger weights, encouraging simpler models that generalize better to new data. Additionally, we employ a learning rate scheduler, specifically ReduceLROnPlateau 29, which decreases the learning rate when the validation loss plateaus, enhancing the model's ability to converge to a better local minimum. The network is trained using backpropagation, systematically updating weights to minimize loss across a series of epochs, thus refining its ability to classify multiple cardiac anomalies from ECG data.

Evaluation Metrics. To assess the performance of our model, we use the F1 score and Hamming loss as our primary metrics. The F1 score is crucial for understanding the model's accuracy in predicting each label, as it balances the precision and recall, which is vital in medical diagnostics where both false positives and false negatives carry significant consequences. Hamming loss is particularly appropriate for our multilabel setting as it measures the fraction of the wrong labels to the total number of labels, providing insight into the overall prediction error across all categories. This metric is essential for evaluating the model’s ability to handle the intricacies of multilabel classification, ensuring that our model not only recognizes the patterns effectively but also minimizes misclassifications across the numerous possible conditions. These metrics are instrumental in tuning our CNN to achieve high precision and recall, ensuring that our model reliably identifies multiple cardiac abnormalities from ECG data.

Hyperparameter Tuning. Hyperparameter tuning is always an issue in any machine learning project. For our CNN, we considered tuning the learning rate and the weight decay. To determine good values for these variables, we used a subset of the data that was not used for training (i.e., our validation set). We designed a space-filling computer experiment using the maximum projection design (MaxPro) as implemented in the R package MaxPro (created by Dr. Roshan Joseph here at Georgia Tech). 30 31 The 10-run design (an additional pre-experimental run was also included) allowed us to experiment with several different combinations of the hyperparameters while ensuring that the design space was evenly covered as shown in Fig. 12 below.

3.3. CNN Results

3.3.1 Hyperparameter Experiment Results

In our study, the hyperparameter tuning for the convolutional neural network (CNN) was methodically executed using the MaxPro experimental design, as described in 2.4.2, in order to explore a variety of combinations of learning rate and weight decay. This approach, leveraging a subset of data reserved for validation, facilitated a structured exploration across the hyperparameter space. We conducted a total of 10 experimental runs—each defined by a unique pair of learning rate and weight decay values generated from the MaxPro design. The consistency across runs was maintained by fixing other hyperparameters, including the number of epochs, batch size, scheduler factor and patience, as well as the fixed count of patients (5000 patients) and labels (5 labels).

Figure 20. Hyperparameter experiment results

The effectiveness of each hyperparameter combination was assessed based on the final validation losses, which were comprehensively recorded and depicted in Fig. 20. This figure includes a contour plot visualizing the hyperparameter design space, offering a clear view of how variations in learning rate and weight decay influenced model performance.

Further analysis was performed using kriging, a form of Gaussian Process Regression with a radial basis function kernel. This statistical technique allowed us to interpolate the sparse data points from our 10-run design to estimate a continuous surface of validation losses across the hyperparameter space. This predictive modeling was crucial in identifying the optimal settings for our CNN.

The outcome of this optimization process determined that the best hyperparameter settings for our model were a learning rate of 0.001 and a weight decay of 0.00697. These parameters were subsequently adopted for all further experiments and training, ensuring that the model training was optimized for both performance and generalizability. However, one notable issue would be that the optimal hyperparameter values were at the edge of the search space, as can be seen in the contour plot of Fig. 20.

Throughout the training over 100 epochs, initial results indicated a similar pattern of loss reduction and accuracy improvement as observed in the four-label setup. Starting with an initial training loss of 0.5000 and a training Hamming loss of 0.2237, the model managed to reduce these values to 0.1842 and 0.0885, respectively, by epoch 100. The validation loss mirrored this improvement, decreasing from 0.4236 to 0.2096, and the validation Hamming loss improved from 0.1844 to 0.0989.

However, compared to the four-label model, the six-label configuration demonstrated slightly higher loss values and lower accuracy metrics, suggesting increased difficulty in model training due to the added complexity of two more labels. For instance, the final F1-score on the training set reached 0.7327, which is notably lower than the 0.9541 achieved in the four-label case. Similarly, the validation F1-score peaked at 0.6750, lower than the 0.8990 seen previously, indicating challenges in generalization with more conditions to classify.

This comparative reduction in performance metrics could be attributed to the inherent challenge of distinguishing between a greater number of similar cardiac conditions, where symptoms and ECG patterns may overlap more significantly. It also reflects the model’s strain in learning more complex patterns effectively, necessitating perhaps more sophisticated or different approaches to feature extraction and classification.

Moreover, the fluctuation in metrics like F1-score and validation losses across epochs was more pronounced in this setup, suggesting that the model faced greater challenges in stabilizing learning gains across the additional labels. These oscillations in performance metrics highlight the potential need for further optimization in aspects like network architecture or training regimen when scaling up the number of labels in multi-label classification tasks.

Overall, the results from the six-label trial of the CNN clearly demonstrate that while the model maintains a capability to learn and improve with more complex datasets, there is a noticeable impact on performance metrics. This underscores the necessity for tailored approaches in model training and architecture design to better handle increased classification complexity and ensure robust performance across a broader range of conditions.

Performance comparison of 2D variation maps and 2D feature variation maps. When discussing machine learning models applied to complex signal processing tasks, such as ECG analysis, there's often a necessary trade-off among model complexity, performance, and computational efficiency. The disparity in performance between 2D variation maps composed of all 5000 features per ECG signal versus 2D feature variation maps composed of a reduced set of features (e.g., 60 features per ECG signal) exemplifies this trade-off. Reducing the number of features from 5000 leads to significant dimensionality reduction in the input data, which may omit subtle yet critical variations in ECG signals that are crucial for accurate classification or anomaly detection. The selected subset of features in these reduced 2D feature variation maps might not capture the complete diagnostic information present in the full dataset, potentially overlooking important ECG signal characteristics, such as minor heart rhythm fluctuations or transient features indicative of conditions like arrhythmias.

On the other hand, using a reduced subset of features simplifies the model’s structure, which generally leads to faster training times due to fewer parameters needing updates during each learning iteration. This reduced complexity also enhances computational efficiency, requiring less computational power and memory. Such simplified models are particularly advantageous in scenarios where resources are limited, including mobile health monitoring or real-time patient screening in clinical settings.

  1. Conclusions & Future Work

In our LSTM implementation, the possibility of adopting a bi-directional LSTM architecture presents a compelling opportunity for future enhancements. Bi-directional LSTMs, by processing information both forwards and backwards in time, could potentially identify and utilize patterns in the ECG data that are not apparent in a forward-only analysis. This method might especially improve the detection of subtle ECG abnormalities that manifest more clearly in reverse sequence analysis. Although the impact on accuracy remains to be validated, the bi-directional approach holds substantial promise for more nuanced understanding and prediction capabilities. Additionally, the challenge of uneven label distribution could be addressed more effectively in future models. We initially balanced the label distribution manually for the LSTM, but alternative strategies such as oversampling rare conditions or using logarithmic adjustments to label frequency could refine the model’s learning process. These methods would allow the model to maintain a realistic representation of common conditions while enhancing its sensitivity to rarer anomalies, potentially leading to improvements in predictive performance and clinical relevance.

Transitioning to the convolutional neural network (CNN), our results have demonstrated its efficacy in multi-label classification of 12-lead ECGs, with the model showing improved accuracy and adaptability over 100 epochs of training. The increase from four to six labels, however, highlighted challenges related to scaling as evidenced by fluctuating validation losses and a slight dip in F1-score, indicating that model complexity and task difficulty are closely linked.

Looking ahead, the pursuit of more complex network architectures and deeper CNN models could potentially yield better feature extraction capabilities, which are crucial for distinguishing subtle differences between similar cardiac conditions. Exploring advanced regularization techniques and alternative optimization algorithms may also assist in managing increased model complexity and achieving more stable training outcomes. Furthermore, applying transfer learning or semi-supervised learning techniques could capitalize on unlabeled data, which is often plentiful in medical datasets, to enhance the model's generalizability to new, unseen data. Expanding the dataset size and diversity is another critical step that would likely boost the robustness and diagnostic accuracy of the model, ensuring it performs well across various patient demographics and clinical scenarios.

In conclusion, while our current models provide a solid foundation and promising results, the path forward involves a strategic blend of advanced modeling techniques, richer data handling strategies, and leveraging computational innovations to fully realize the potential of machine learning in cardiac diagnostics.
